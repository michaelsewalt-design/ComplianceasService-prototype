1 · What Are Conflicts of Interest?

A conflict of interest (COI) arises when a person or organisation has competing loyalties or interests that could improperly influence their professional judgment or decision-making. In financial services, COI management is a cornerstone of integrity — the obligation to act honestly, carefully and in the client's best interest.

Key principle: A conflict exists whenever the interests of a financial undertaking, its employees, tied agents or clients diverge, creating a risk of detriment to one or more clients or to the integrity of the market.

Conflicts may be actual (already occurring), potential (likely to arise), or perceived (appear to exist to a reasonable observer). All three categories must be identified, recorded and managed.

2 · Types of Conflicts You Will Encounter

Personal Conflicts

  • Personal account dealing (PAD) — trading in financial instruments for personal benefit using knowledge gained at work. Pre-clearance and holding periods apply.
  • Gifts & entertainment — receiving or offering hospitality that could compromise objectivity. Most firms set thresholds (e.g. €50–100) above which approval or refusal is required.
  • Outside business activities (OBA) — side jobs, board seats or advisory roles that create divided loyalties. Pre-approval required.
  • Relationship conflicts — family, friends or close relations as clients, suppliers or counterparties.

Organisational Conflicts

  • Proprietary trading vs. client order execution
  • Investment research independence vs. corporate finance revenue
  • Cross-selling incentives and remuneration structures
  • Advising both buyer and seller in the same transaction

3 · Key Policies You Must Know

Gift & Entertainment Policy

  • Gifts above the threshold must be reported or declined
  • Entertainment during tender/procurement processes is typically prohibited
  • All gifts received or given must be logged in the central gift register
  • Cash and cash equivalents are always prohibited

Personal Account Dealing (PAD)

  • Pre-clearance required before executing personal trades
  • Minimum holding periods (e.g. 30–90 days)
  • Restricted lists block instruments related to client mandates or deals
  • Annual portfolio and brokerage account declarations

Outside Business Activities

  • Pre-approval for any paid or unpaid external role
  • Annual re-confirmation of all existing OBAs
  • Assessment of time commitment, reputational risk and potential conflicts

4 · The Banker's Oath & Your Obligations

Since 2015, all employees in the Dutch banking sector must take the Banker's Oath (Bankierseed). Key commitments include:

  • I will perform my duties with integrity and care
  • I will carefully weigh all interests — clients, shareholders, employees and society
  • I will keep the interests of the client central
  • I will not abuse my knowledge
  • I will act openly and with integrity and be accountable for my conduct

Violations can lead to disciplinary proceedings before Stichting Tuchtrecht Banken, with sanctions ranging from a reprimand to a 3-year professional ban.

5 · When in Doubt: Escalate

If you identify or suspect a conflict of interest:

  • Disclose the conflict to your manager and/or compliance immediately
  • Do not take any action that could benefit from the conflict before it is resolved
  • Use the whistleblowing channel if you suspect someone is suppressing a COI disclosure
  • Remember: silence can amount to complicity
Rule of thumb: If you would not be comfortable seeing your action described in a newspaper headline, it is probably a conflict that needs to be disclosed.

1 · Regulatory Framework for COI Management

Wft & Bgfo

The Wet op het financieel toezicht (Wft) and Besluit Gedragstoezicht financiële ondernemingen (Bgfo) require every financial undertaking to maintain a systematic integrity risk analysis (SIRA) that covers COI as a risk category, implement policies and procedures to identify, prevent, manage and record conflicts, and disclose unmanageable conflicts to the client before providing a service.

MiFID II

For investment firms, MiFID II (Articles 23, 16(3)) and Delegated Regulation 2017/565 (Articles 33–35) impose detailed requirements: maintain a COI policy proportionate to size and complexity, establish information barriers where necessary, maintain a COI register, and ensure remuneration structures do not create conflicts.

DNB / AFM Expectations

DNB expects controlled and ethical business operations with COI management as a core component. AFM monitors quality of COI policies, inducement arrangements, PAD controls and independence of compliance, risk and internal audit functions.

2 · Information Barriers (Chinese Walls)

  • Physical separation — separate offices/floors for conflicted departments
  • Logical separation — restricted IT access, separate shared drives and databases
  • Wall-crossing procedures — formal process with compliance oversight for bringing someone "over the wall"
  • Clean teams / ring-fencing — restricting information to a defined group during sensitive transactions

The compliance function must maintain documentation of all wall-crossings including: the names of persons involved, the reason, date/time, and conditions under which they may return to their normal duties.

3 · Whistleblowing Framework

The Dutch Whistleblowers Authority Act (Wet bescherming klokkenluiders, 2023 update implementing EU Directive 2019/1937) requires:

  • Internal reporting channel accessible to all employees
  • Appointment of a trusted person (vertrouwenspersoon)
  • Protection against retaliation (dismissal, demotion, harassment)
  • Diligent and confidential investigation of reports
  • Feedback to the reporter within a reasonable timeframe

4 · COI Register & SIRA Integration

A compliant COI register must document: the identified conflict, parties involved, assessment of materiality, management measures applied, and residual risk acceptance. This register feeds into the institution's SIRA, ensuring integrity risks are systematically captured alongside AML, sanctions and fraud risks.

Inducements & Remuneration

Under MiFID II, inducements are only permissible if they enhance the quality of service to the client and do not impair the firm's duty to act in the client's best interest. Variable remuneration structures must be assessed for COI implications, particularly where they incentivise sales of in-house products over better alternatives.

1 · Board-Level Integrity Governance

Under the Wft and Bgfo, the management board bears ultimate responsibility for ensuring the institution operates with integrity. This includes:

  • Approving and periodically reviewing the COI policy and integrity framework
  • Setting the tone from the top — visibly demonstrating ethical leadership
  • Ensuring adequate resources for the compliance function
  • Maintaining personal compliance with the Banker's Oath
  • Overseeing the SIRA and ensuring COI is properly captured

2 · Personal Liability & The Banker's Oath

As a board member, you have personally sworn the Banker's Oath and can face personal consequences for integrity failures:

Stichting Tuchtrecht Banken can sanction individual board members. DNB can issue personal fines and revoke fit & proper assessments. In extreme cases, criminal prosecution for culpable negligence in maintaining ethical business operations is possible.

The board must also ensure that whistleblowing reports reaching the executive level are handled with urgency, confidentiality and independence — particularly where they implicate fellow board members or senior management.

3 · Remuneration & Incentive Structures

The board is responsible for ensuring remuneration policies do not create perverse incentives:

  • Variable pay must not incentivise behaviour contrary to client interests
  • Sales targets must be balanced with quality and compliance metrics
  • Claw-back provisions should cover integrity and compliance breaches
  • The remuneration committee should assess COI implications annually

4 · Information Barriers & Governance Conflicts

At board level, conflicts often arise from:

  • Dual roles — serving on boards of group entities that may have conflicting interests
  • Related-party transactions — ensuring proper recusal procedures exist
  • Supervisory board oversight — ensuring the supervisory board has adequate independence
  • M&A and strategic decisions — managing conflicts where board members have personal interests

Robust board-level recusal procedures and a comprehensive register of interests are essential governance controls.

5 · What DNB & AFM Expect

Regulators assess the board's integrity framework against these criteria:

  • Is the COI policy comprehensive, up-to-date and proportionate?
  • Does the board demonstrably set the tone from the top?
  • Are adequate resources allocated to the compliance function?
  • Is there independent oversight via internal audit?
  • Are whistleblowing reports handled effectively and without retaliation?
  • Does the SIRA adequately capture and assess integrity risks?
  • Are enforcement trends and regulatory expectations actively monitored?
Board accountability: DNB and AFM increasingly hold individual board members accountable for systemic integrity failures. A passive stance ("I didn't know") is no longer an acceptable defence.
