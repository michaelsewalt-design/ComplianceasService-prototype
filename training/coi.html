Conflict of Interest & Integrity — Wft, Bgfo, MiFID II

Conflicts of interest arise when personal, financial or organisational interests can improperly influence professional judgement. In Dutch financial services, firms must identify, prevent, manage and document these conflicts under the Wft, Bgfo and, where relevant, MiFID II. The goal is not only client protection, but also safeguarding market integrity and ethical behaviour across the institution.

🎯
What Is a Conflict of Interest?
Wft / Bgfo / MiFID II Art. 23
▼

A conflict of interest exists when competing loyalties or incentives can influence your judgement, decisions or recommendations. Conflicts can be actual, potential or perceived. All three matter because even the appearance of bias can damage trust, client outcomes and market integrity.

In financial institutions, conflicts can arise between the firm and a client, between two clients, between an employee and a client, or between business lines with different incentives. The institution must have a framework to recognise these risks early and apply controls before harm occurs.

Example

A banker has a personal financial interest in a company while also advising clients on that same company. Even if no misconduct has yet occurred, the overlap creates a conflict that must be disclosed, assessed and controlled.

ANALYST

Watch for these patterns:

Personal interests that could affect how you treat a client or supplier

Gifts, hospitality or personal relationships influencing professional decisions

Requests to “keep things informal” instead of disclosing and escalating

COMPLIANCE

The compliance framework must cover identification, assessment, documentation, mitigation and escalation of COI risks. Under MiFID II, firms need a proportionate COI policy, governance oversight and clear rules for disclosures where conflicts cannot be fully mitigated.

MANAGEMENT

The board bears ultimate responsibility for ensuring that the organisation operates with integrity. This includes approving the COI framework, setting the tone from the top and making sure conflicts are visible in the SIRA and broader governance model.

🔍
Types of Conflicts You Will Encounter
PAD / Gifts / OBA / Information Barriers
▼

Common conflict categories include personal account dealing, gifts and entertainment, outside business activities, relationship conflicts, and information barrier breaches. These can affect objectivity, client treatment, independence and confidentiality.

Key categories

Examples include trading in securities connected to your work, accepting expensive hospitality during a tender, serving on an external board, helping a close relative as a client, or receiving confidential deal information across a Chinese wall.

ANALYST

At front-line level, the most relevant risks are often practical and personal: gifts and entertainment, personal trades, family or friend relationships, and side roles that create divided loyalties.

COMPLIANCE

Information barriers require physical, logical and procedural controls. Wall-crossing must be documented, justified and monitored. Compliance should be able to reconstruct who received what information, when, and under which restrictions.

MANAGEMENT

At board level, conflicts often arise through dual mandates, related-party transactions, strategic decisions and oversight gaps. Senior leaders must be transparent about their interests and follow formal recusal procedures.

📋
Key Policies, Registers and Controls
COI Policy / PAD / Gifts / OBA
▼

A robust integrity framework relies on clear rules and documented controls. Typical pillars are the gifts & entertainment policy, personal account dealing rules, outside business activity approvals, the COI register, and information barrier procedures.

Controls should not exist only on paper. Employees need to know when pre-clearance is required, when to refuse an offer, how to disclose a conflict, and how decisions are logged for audit and supervisory review.

Example

A gift may seem harmless in isolation, but if it coincides with a procurement process or a client decision, it can become an integrity risk that requires refusal or escalation.

ANALYST

You should know the practical boundaries: when gifts exceed thresholds, when PAD pre-clearance is required, when external roles need approval, and when you must step aside from a file because of a personal relationship.

COMPLIANCE

The COI register should document the conflict, involved parties, severity assessment, mitigating controls and residual risk. This should feed into the SIRA so integrity risks are systematically captured alongside AML, sanctions and fraud risks.

MANAGEMENT

Boards must ensure remuneration and incentive structures do not reward behaviour that is misaligned with client interests or market integrity. Variable pay, sales targets and external mandates all require governance oversight.

🛡️
Banker’s Oath, Culture and Whistleblowing
Bankierseed / Wet bescherming klokkenluiders
▼

The Banker’s Oath requires employees to act with integrity, care and accountability. This is not symbolic: it shapes expected conduct and can lead to disciplinary consequences if breached. Ethical culture also depends on safe escalation and effective whistleblowing arrangements.

Employees must be able to raise concerns without retaliation. Under Dutch whistleblowing protections, firms need internal reporting channels, confidentiality, diligent follow-up and protection against adverse treatment.

Example

If a junior colleague reports that a manager may be suppressing a conflict disclosure, you cannot ignore it or tell them to “leave it alone.” The concern must be taken seriously and routed through the proper channels.

ANALYST

Your personal obligation is to act openly, disclose concerns, and avoid using your position or knowledge for private gain. Silence can itself become an integrity failure when you know something should be escalated.

COMPLIANCE

Compliance must ensure that whistleblowing channels, trusted person arrangements and anti-retaliation controls are effective in practice. Record-keeping, confidentiality and timely feedback are essential.

MANAGEMENT

Senior management is accountable for tone from the top. If staff do not trust escalation channels or fear retaliation, regulators will view that as a governance failure rather than an isolated HR issue.

⚠️
Escalation, Enforcement and Consequences
AFM / DNB / Tuchtrecht
▼

When in doubt, the rule is simple: disclose, document and escalate. Conflicts that are hidden or handled informally create regulatory, disciplinary and reputational risk for both individuals and the institution.

Consequences can range from internal disciplinary action to supervisory enforcement by DNB or AFM, civil claims, reputational damage, and in some cases personal sanctions under the Banker’s Oath framework.

Rule of thumb

If you would not be comfortable explaining the situation to compliance, the board or a regulator, you should not be resolving it informally yourself.

ANALYST

If you identify a conflict, stop, disclose it to your manager or compliance, and do not take further action that could benefit from the conflict until guidance is given.

COMPLIANCE

Enforcement risk increases when the organisation cannot evidence decisions, managing actions and oversight. Regulators expect clear documentation, consistent treatment and timely escalation.

MANAGEMENT

The board should periodically review COI incidents, breaches and whistleblowing patterns to assess whether the integrity framework is working in practice, and whether incentive structures or leadership behaviour need adjustment.
